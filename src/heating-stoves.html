WOOD VS. GAS

What's Better for Me? Gas Stoves vs. Wood Stoves

Choosing between a gas stove and a wood stove is a big decision. Both deliver powerful, room-filling warmth and serve as a striking focal point...but they differ in how they run, what they cost to operate, and how much hands-on work they require.

Here's how gas and wood stoves compare across the factors that matter most:

  • ◆ Convenience & maintenance: Gas turns on with a switch or remote and produces no ash, while wood requires loading, kindling, and regular ash removal.
  • ◆ Fuel cost & availability: Cordwood is often cheaper per BTU if you have access to seasoned hardwood, while natural gas and propane offer predictable monthly costs.
  • ◆ Ambiance: Wood gives you a real flame, crackle, and aroma, while gas provides a clean, consistent fire view without the smoke or smell.
  • ◆ Venting requirements: Wood stoves need a Class A chimney or lined masonry flue, while direct-vent gas models can vent horizontally through an exterior wall.
  • ◆ Lifestyle fit: wood rewards homeowners who enjoy the ritual of building a fire, while gas suits busy households that want instant, low-effort warmth

Blackburns helps homeowners weigh all of the factors during an in-home consultation, then expertly installs both gas and wood stoves to manufacturer specs and local code.

COMMON QUESTIONS

Heating Stoves: Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a chimney for a heating stove?
Every heating stove needs a safe path for combustion byproducts to exit your home, but the venting requirements depend on the stove type:
  • Wood-burning stoves require a Class A insulated chimney or a properly lined existing masonry chimney sized to the stove's flue collar. Improper sizing causes draft problems and creosote buildup.
  • Direct-vent gas stoves can vent horizontally through an exterior wall using a coaxial pipe – no traditional chimney required. This opens up installation in rooms without existing flues.
  • B-vent and pellet stoves have their own specific venting rules, with shorter clearance requirements than wood stoves.
  • Existing chimneys are inspected before reuse to confirm condition, sizing, and the need for a stainless-steel liner.
During your in-home consultation we evaluate your space, identify the safest venting route, and provide a clear plan that meets local code.
How much does a heating stove installation cost?
Total project cost depends on the stove you choose plus the venting and site work needed. A typical project includes several components:
  • The stove itself: Entry-level models start around the lower end of the market, while premium cast-iron and soapstone stoves are a higher investment.
  • Venting and chimney work: New Class A chimney runs, stainless-steel liners, or direct-vent pipe kits each carry different material and labor costs.
  • Hearth pad and clearances: Non-combustible floor protection and wall shielding may be required to meet code.
  • Gas line or electrical: Gas stoves need a fuel line, and some models require a 110V outlet for blowers or controls.
  • Permits and inspection: These are included in our quote so there are no surprises.
After a brief in-home assessment, we provide a transparent, itemized quote so you know exactly what you're paying for.
Are heating stoves safe?
Yes – modern heating stoves are extremely safe when properly installed, maintained, and operated. Safety comes from layering several protections:
  • EPA-certified appliances burn cleaner and hotter, reducing creosote and emissions.
  • Code-compliant clearances from combustible walls, floors, and ceilings – verified during installation.
  • Proper venting sized to the stove and inspected for draft, joints, and termination height.
  • Carbon monoxide and smoke detectors on every level of the home, especially near sleeping areas.
  • Annual inspections and sweeping to remove creosote, check gaskets and door seals, and catch wear before it becomes a hazard.
Our CSIA-certified technicians follow NFPA 211 and all local building codes so your stove operates safely from day one.
How often does a heating stove need maintenance?
Maintenance frequency depends on stove type and how often you use it, but every stove benefits from a yearly check-up:
  • Wood-burning stoves should be swept and inspected annually – and more often if you burn more than a cord of wood per season or notice glaze creosote.
  • Gas stoves need yearly inspection of the burner, pilot, thermocouple, venting, and glass gaskets to maintain safe operation and efficiency.
  • Pellet stoves require regular cleaning of the burn pot, ash pan, and exhaust pathway – typically every few weeks during heavy use, plus an annual professional service.
  • Daily/weekly user care: empty ash, check door gaskets, keep the glass clean, and burn only seasoned hardwood (under 20% moisture) in wood stoves.
We offer scheduled maintenance plans so your stove stays safe, efficient, and ready for every heating season.
