How Do I Know if My Chimney Liner Is Failing?

Your chimney's liner is the barrier between hot gases and the masonry structure of your home. Over time, exposure to heat, moisture, and acidic combustion byproducts causes liners to crack, deteriorate, or separate from the chimney walls.

Signs your liner needs attention:

  • ◆Visible cracks or gaps in the flue when inspected with a camera
  • ◆Smoke backing up into the home or lingering odors
  • ◆Water stains or moisture around the fireplace or chimney interior
  • ◆Rust or deterioration visible in the flue
  • ◆Installation of a new wood stove, insert, or heating appliance
  • ◆Chimney has never been lined or the existing liner is over 30 years old

When a liner fails, dangerous combustion gases—including carbon monoxide—can seep into your living spaces. Creosote and moisture can penetrate the surrounding masonry, causing water damage and structural deterioration. And poor draft performance makes your fireplace or stove work harder and less efficiently. The good news: a new stainless steel liner restores your chimney's functionality and safety in a single installation. What's the Main Purpose of a Chimney Liner?

A correctly installed, code-compliant chimney liner does far more than pass an inspection. It protects your family and your home while enabling your fireplace or stove to perform as intended.

Prevents Carbon Monoxide Leaks

A sealed liner keeps dangerous gases venting safely outside, not into your home.

Reduces Chimney Fire Risk

Improves Draft and Performance

Protects Chimney Masonry

A liner shields the brick and mortar from corrosive gases and moisture, extending chimney life.

Meets Building Codes

Enables Appliance Upgrades

COMMON QUESTIONS

Chimney Relining — Frequently Asked Questions

What does a chimney liner do?

A chimney liner is a protective inner channel–typically stainless steel, aluminum, or clay tile–that runs the full height of your chimney and safely directs combustion byproducts up and out of your home.

Key functions include:

  • Containing heat, smoke, and gases so they vent outside instead of seeping into living spaces
  • Protecting the brick and mortar from corrosive acids and moisture
  • Improving draft and appliance efficiency
  • Reducing the risk of chimney fires by limiting creosote contact with masonry
What types of chimney liners are available?

There are three main options, each suited to different appliances and chimney conditions:

  • Stainless steel – the most common and versatile choice; works with wood, gas, oil, and pellet appliances; typically lasts 15–20+ years
  • Aluminum – lower cost and lighter weight, but only approved for certain medium-efficiency gas appliances
  • Clay tile – the original liner in many older masonry chimneys; durable but prone to cracking from thermal shock and difficult to repair section-by-section

We inspect your chimney and appliance before recommending the right option for your home.

How long does a chimney liner last?

Lifespan depends on the material, how often you burn, and your maintenance habits:

  • Stainless steel: typically 15–20+ years; premium liners often come with lifetime warranties
  • Aluminum: 5–15 years, gas appliances only
  • Clay tile: can last decades if undamaged, but cracks significantly shorten useful life

Annual sweeping, prompt repairs, and a properly fitted chimney cap all help extend liner life.

What are the signs that my liner needs repair or replacement?

Watch for any of the following–any one of them warrants a closer look:

  • Smoke spilling back into the room or lingering fireplace odors
  • Visible cracks, gaps, or rust seen on a camera inspection
  • Water stains, efflorescence, or moisture inside the firebox or on nearby walls
  • Pieces of tile or debris falling into the fireplace
  • Reduced draft or difficulty starting and maintaining a fire
  • An appliance change (new stove, insert, or higher-efficiency furnace) that requires a different liner size

A camera inspection is the only reliable way to confirm liner condition.

Can a damaged liner cause carbon monoxide issues?

Yes–and it's one of the most serious risks of a failing chimney. Cracks, gaps, or deteriorated joints can allow carbon monoxide and other combustion gases to leak through the masonry and into living spaces, where they are odorless and potentially deadly.

Recommended steps:

  • Schedule a camera inspection if you've noticed soot, odors, or backdraft
  • Install working CO detectors on every level of your home
  • Replace damaged liners promptly–don't postpone
